Decreased cross-presentation by antigen-presenting cells induces the scarcity of
tumour-reactive T cells within the tumour bed, rendering in situ T cell rejuvenation
through immunogenicity reprogramming highly desirable yet challenging1–3
. Here we
developed an intratumoural vaccination chimera (iVAC) to reprogram tumour cells
into an antigen-presenting state (APC-like tumour cells) with restored anti-tumour
immunity. The iVAC chimeras consist of a covalently engineered PD-L1 degrader
conjugated to immunogenic antigens, which could relieve immune checkpoint
inhibition while enforcing the cross-presentation of exogenous antigens. Functionally,
the iVAC-induced antigen processing and presentation elicited potent tumour killing
through reactivation of resident antigen-specific CD8+
 T cells, which simultaneously
remodelled the tumour microenvironment to promote durable tumour-specific
immunity. Extending this strategy, we used iVAC with a cytomegalovirus (CMV)-derived
antigen to activate CMV-specific T cells against breast cancer in vitro, in a humanized
mouse model as well as in a patient-derived tumour model. This study establishes a
foundation for chemically reprogramming cancer cells within tumour beds to endow
APC-like functions, providing an avenue for stimulating anti-tumour immunity.
Tumours use various mechanisms to reduce immunogenicity and evade
immune surveillance1,2
, including the overexpression of inhibitory
checkpoint proteins, neoantigen editing and impairments in antigen
presentation. Restoring anti-tumour immunity fundamentally depends
on supplementing and activating tumour-reactive T cells3
 by enhancing
tumour antigen presentation. Immune checkpoint blockade therapy
has transformed cancer treatment4
; however, response rates remain
limited due to the deficiency or dysfunction of tumour-specific cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs)5
. Although adoptive T cell transfer and
cancer vaccines have been designed to increase CTL presence and
bolster anti-tumour responses, vaccine-induced CTLs often succumb to
immunosuppressive conditions within the tumour microenvironment
(TME), necessitating combination with other therapies6–9
. An integrated
vaccination strategy to replenish and reactivate intratumoural T cells
both at the generation and effector phases remains highly desirable
but challenging.
Recent evidence indicates that the tumour bed can act as a site for
T cell priming and activation10,11, initiating the cancer-immunity cycle,
as highlighted by the essential role of tertiary lymphoid structures in
supporting anti-tumour immunity12–14. During antigen-specific T cell
activation, APCs bridge tumour antigens with T cell receptors (TCRs)
on CTLs15. In vaccine development, these APCs—primarily dendritic
cells (DCs)—are targeted to deliver and present exogenous antigens
in lymph nodes and tumours16. However, tumours contain abundant
cancer cells but few functional APCs, particularly DCs15,17. Moreover,
most tumour-resident T cells are bystanders, recognizing non-tumour
antigens (such as viral epitopes) rather than tumour-specific antigens18–21. Reprogramming tumour cells into APCs (APC-like tumour
cells) capable of cross-presenting tumour antigens could facilitate
direct T cell activation and expansion within the tumour bed. Effective
APC-like tumour cells would need to (1) internalize exogenous antigens;
(2) process and present them; and (3) activate antigen-specific T cells22.
Emerging strategies have explored converting tumour cells into APCs
through enforced expression of specific transcription factors to drive
reprogramming23,24. However, these genetic methods can be inefficient,
heterogeneous and raise safety concerns, such as oncogene activation
and genomic instability, highlighting the need for a direct, controllable
approach through exogenous small molecules or antibodies25.
Inspired by cellular reprogramming, we hypothesized that coupling
intracellular protein degradation machinery with exogenous antigen
processing and presentation could endow tumour cells with APC-like
functions, which may serve as the vaccination targets for intratumoural
T cell supplement. To implement this strategy, we established an immunogenicity reprogramming approach with an iVAC to enforce antigen
presentation in tumour cells. This would take advantage of immune
checkpoint protein degradation to simultaneously alleviate immune
suppression and boost tumour antigenicity with a remodelled TME, culminating in tumour-specific T cell activation and durable therapeutic
efficacy. Our iVAC uses a covalently engineered nanobody (GlueBody)
to enhance target engagement, promoting rapid PD-L1 degradation

Design and validation of iVAC
APC-like tumour cells must satisfy three essential criteria: (1) antigen
uptake and internalization; (2) antigen processing and presentation;
(3) activation of antigen-specific T cells. To overcome the time-intensive
nature of cell-fate reprogramming with transcription factors, which can
take several days, we used biomacromolecules to chemically convert
cancer cells to an APC-like status, directly targeting these three functional characteristics to restore tumour immunogenicity and potentiate tumour therapies (Fig. 1a). In contrast to conventional APCs that
rely on pattern-recognition receptors for antigen internalization, our
approach used a lysosome-targeting receptor-independent membrane
protein degrader through the conjugated cell-penetrating peptide
(CPP), namely, the covalent nanobody-based chimera (GlueBody–CPP).
This method uses the endosomal–lysosomal degradation pathway after
CPP-mediated internalization of the target–chimera complex, together
with associated antigenic peptide presentation machinery. By coupling
antigenic peptides with the degrader, we engineered chimeric entities
to facilitate forced presentation of exogenous antigens (Fig. 1b). The
efficiency of this antigenicity reprogramming therefore depends on
both the degrader’s targeting affinity and its ability to deliver antigens
into tumour cells for cross-presentation.
To optimize degrader engagement with membrane targets, we
designed a next-generation covalent nanobody that forms irreversible
bonds with membrane proteins through in situ cross-linking. We optimized the proximity-reactive warhead fluorosulfate-L-tyrosine (FSY)
by synthesizing fluoride-modified FSY variants (FnFSYs) to react with
target residue His69 on human PD-L1 (Fig. 1c) with enhanced reactivity
through sulfur fluoride exchange26–28. Testing these fluoride-substituted
phenyl sulfurofluoridates with imidazole as a model confirmed that the
di-ortho-fluoro variant yielded the highest reactivity (Extended Data
Fig. 1a–c). To produce FnFSYs (Fig. 1d), we used tyrosine phenol lyase
(TPL)-catalysed biosynthesis and successfully obtained mono- and
di-fluoro FSYs (Extended Data Fig. 1d). Reactivity studies revealed that
3,5-difluoro FSY had optimal yields (Extended Data Fig. 1e–g).
We next assessed the cross-linking efficiency of FnFSYs with PD-L1 in
the anti-PD-L1 nanobody KN035 (Nb-PD-L1) using a genetic code expansion strategy29,30. After selecting the orthogonal aminoacyl tRNA synthetase with the V366A mutant (Extended Data Fig. 2), we synthesized
PD-L1-targeting GlueBodies and verified FnFSY incorporation at site
Leu108 of Nb-PD-L1. In vitro tests showed that GlueBody (2,3-difluoro
FSY) had the highest PD-L1 coupling efficiency within 30 min (Extended
Data Fig. 3a), achieving over 40% cross-linking within 30 min and 80%
after 2 h (Fig. 1e). This GlueBody variant was renamed GlueBody2 and
was conjugated to functional peptides (CPP for degradation and CPPOVA for degradation-coupled antigen presentation) by sortase A.
In HCC2935 cells, the GlueBody2–CPP construct degraded 92% of PD-L1
at 100 nM within 4 h (Fig. 1f,g and Extended Data Fig. 3b), while demonstrating a more potent ability to restore T cell function as confirmed
by a PD-L1–PD-1 luciferase reporter assay31 (Fig. 1h).
GlueBody2–CPP was further linked to the OVA257–264 (SIINFEKL) antigenic peptide through different peptide linkers (D1–D4; Fig. 1i), and the
following antigen presentation test on human PD-L1 (hPD-L1)/MC38
model cells identified the D4 variant, termed iVAC, as the most efficient
chimera for cathepsin recognition and antigen processing (Fig. 1i and
Extended Data Fig. 3c). This efficiency was shown to be dependent
on hPD-L1 expression (Extended Data Fig. 3d). Comparative analysis
indicated that iVAC exhibited similar efficiency in target degradation
and T cell reactivation as GlueBody2–CPP (Extended Data Fig. 3e,f).
To validate the use of targeted antigen presentation by PD-L1 degradation in more physiologically relevant settings, PD-L1 humanized
transgenic C57 mice bearing the hPD-L1endo/MC38 cell derived xenograft
that express the endogenous level of hPD-L1 was used for systematic
analysis (Extended Data Fig. 4a–f). The obtained results demonstrated
that the endogenous PD-L1 expression level is sufficient to mediate
exogenous antigen presentation by iVAC. Meanwhile, the intratumoural
retention and biodistribution of iVAC were evaluated on the same
xenograft models. Notably, iVAC exhibited persistent intratumoural
retention, diminished potential off-tumour effects and negligible toxicity (Fig. 1j and Extended Data Fig. 5a–g). Collectively, iVAC achieved
efficient targeted degradation, exogenous antigen presentation as
well as excellent intratumoural retention—satisfying prerequisites for
APC-like reprogramming (Fig. 1k).
iVAC boosts tumour cross-presentation
We next investigated the mechanism of iVAC-mediated antigen presentation on the cell surface as pMHC-I complexes. Mass spectrometry
(MS)-based immunopeptidomics of iVAC-treated MC38 cells identified
peptides binding predominantly to MHC-I, with OVApep observed at
high levels (Fig. 2a and Supplementary Table 1), demonstrating iVAC’s
potential to facilitate rapid tumour cell antigen uptake and presentation. A time-course study showed that OVApep delivered by iVAC
continued to accumulate over 24 h, while passively bound OVApep dissociated after 6 h (Fig. 2b). Further experiments showed that OVApep
in the medium formed pMHC-I complexes under all conditions, while
iVAC antigen presentation was blocked at 4 °C (Fig. 2c). To delineate
how APC-like cross-presentation operates in tumour cells, we combined

We also assessed the ability of iVAC to enhance tumour cell immunogenicity. In poorly immunogenic B16 cells (hPD-L1/B16) with minimal
surface MHC-I expression, OVApep treatment alone elicited no response
due to low MHC-I levels. However, prolonged iVAC treatment increased
MHC-I molecules and displayed OVApep on the cell surface (Fig. 2g).
RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis of iVAC-treated B16 cells revealed
upregulation of antigen-presenting genes and reduced tumorigenicity
(Fig. 2h,i, Extended Data Fig. 6c and Supplementary Table 2). Consistent with increased immunogenicity, iVAC treatment induced IFNγ and
STING pathway signatures (Extended Data Fig. 6d,e). Gene set enrichment analysis further confirmed activation of innate immune responses
(Extended Data Fig. 6f). Similar changes in antigen-processing transcripts were observed in iVAC-treated MC38 cells (Extended Data
Fig. 6g,h). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that iVAC activates
MHC-I pathways by inducing lysosomal degradation of the chimera–target complex, enabling tumour cells to process and present exogenous
antigens on their surface, conferring APC-like characteristics.
APC-like tumour cells become immunogenic
To determine whether iVAC-reprogrammed APC-like tumour cells
exhibit improved immunogenicity for T cell activation, tumour cells
were treated with 200 nM iVAC for 1 h, washed extensively and cocultured with OT-I CD8+
 T cells preactivated with OVApep and maintained in a memory state (Fig. 3a). After 24 h, reprogrammed APClike tumour cells displayed reduced PD-L1 levels and increased MHC-I
expression, suggesting enhanced immunogenicity (Fig. 3b,c). This
reduction in inhibitory checkpoint proteins combined with increased
antigen presentation may accelerate and amplify OT-I memory CD8+
T cell proliferation. To validate this, CFSE-labelled OT-I memory CD8+
T cells were co-cultured with APC-like tumour cells or bone-marrowderived DCs (BMDCs) at a 10:1 ratio for 48 h. APC-like tumour cells
derived from MC38 cells induced similar T cell activation and expansion
to BMDCs, while free OVApep addition induced only moderate T cell
proliferation in hPD-L1/B16 cells, which inherently have low MHC-I
expression (Fig. 3d,e and Extended Data Fig. 7a,b). Comparable effects
were observed with naive T cell proliferation, where APC-like tumour
cells achieved functionality akin to BMDCs, in contrast to the original
tumour cells (Extended Data Fig. 7c,d).
T cell activation markers, including CD69, CD25 and CD44, along with
cytotoxic markers like granzyme B (GZMB) and PFN, were significantly
upregulated after 12 h in T cells co-cultured with iVAC-reprogrammed
tumour cells, demonstrating effective activation of antigen-specific
T cells (Fig. 3f). This effect exceeded that of extracellular peptide-based
antigen loading (OVApep control). Reprogrammed APC-like tumour
cells also induced stronger responses in naive T cells compared with
direct OVApep addition, although slightly less than professional
BMDCs, probably due to the absence of co-stimulatory molecules
(Extended Data Fig. 7e). Memory T cells activated by iVAC secreted
cytokines such as IFNγ and TNF (Fig. 3g), critical for enhancing T cell
cytotoxicity and further remodelling the TME. Consistent with this
T cell expansion and activation, iVAC-induced immunogenicity reprogramming led to more effective CTL-mediated tumour lysis than OVApep or PD-L1 degrader treatments (Fig. 3h), highlighting the potential
of checkpoint degradation chimeras to deliver exogenous antigen
and convert tumour cells to APC-like entities capable of redirecting
OVA-specific T cells for tumour cell killing.
iVAC elicits durable anti-tumour response

iVAC reprograms human cancer cells
To demonstrate the generality of our strategy, we investigated whether
iVAC could reprogram human breast cancer cells (MDA-MB-231; hereafter, MDA) and stimulate corresponding specific human T cells. Given the
high prevalence of CMV and the significant population of CMV-specific
tumour-resident memory T cells18,21,37, we loaded iVAC with the immunodominant CMV pp65495–503 peptide antigen (NLVPMVATV; hereafter,
NLVpep) to produce iVAC-CMV. The NLVpep was conjugated to the
GlueBody2–CPP degrader through a G4S linker. Immunopeptidomic
analysis of iVAC-CMV-treated MDA cells revealed that the exogenous
CMV antigen was dominantly presented by HLA complexes (Fig. 5a–c).
Notably, peptides derived from endogenous MDA antigens (TP53BP,
PGAP6) were also robustly presented, with a high ranking among
HLA ligands (Fig. 5c and Supplementary Tables 3 and 4). Moreover,
iVAC-reprogrammed MDA cells exhibited enhanced binding affinity
for epitopes (Fig. 5d) and acquired APC-like characteristics.
We next evaluated the ability of reprogrammed MDA cells to activate NLV-specific T  cells. After 6 h of iVAC treatment, MDA cells
were co-cultured for 12 h with JC5 reporter cells transduced with an
NLV-specific TCR. This elicited significantly elevated surface CD69,
indicating T cell activation (Fig. 5e). For improved antigen processing,
several linkers between GlueBody2–CPP and NLVpep were tested and
D8 was found as the most efficient variant for JC5 activation (Fig. 5f and
Supplementary Table 5). We further investigated the therapeutic potential of iVAC-CMV by quantifying NLV-specific T cells in human samples.
Among randomly selected HLA-A*02:01-positive donors, we observed a
high frequency of circulating NLVPMVATV-specific tetramer+
 T cells, with
75% showing response rates exceeding 2%. Authentic NLV-specific T cell
responses were validated through clonal expansion after specific NLVpep
stimulation (Fig. 5g). Subsequently, we evaluated the responsiveness of
these expanded T cells to iVAC-reprogrammed tumour cells. First, after
co-culture with iVAC-treated MDA cells, in vitro expanded NLV-specific
T cells exhibited marked CD69 upregulation (Fig. 5h (left)), albeit with
limited sensitivity. Subsequently, we performed cytokine-release assays,
which confirmed the secretion of IFNγ by these activated T cells after
their exposure to iVAC-reprogrammed tumour cells. This finding demonstrated the occurrence of functional NLV antigen presentation and
the activation of T cells. Responses were observed in 75% (12 out of 16)
of HLA-A*02:01+
 donors (Fig. 5i), indicating iVAC’s ability to stimulate
resident NLV-specific T cells and redirect them against tumours.
In vitro cytotoxicity assays further showed that iVAC-induced checkpoint degradation-coupled antigen presentation led to enhanced
tumour killing compared with PD-L1 degradation alone (Fig. 5j).
Finally, in a humanized mouse model, co-inoculated MDA cells and
PBMCs treated with iVAC significantly slowed tumour growth compared with control and PD-L1 degrader groups (Fig. 5k). Together, these
results highlight the therapeutic promise of iVAC to endow tumour
cells with APC-like properties while simultaneously overcoming
immune checkpoint inhibition, offering a versatile strategy for cancer
treatment.
Efficacy in patient-derived tumour models
To evaluate iVAC in a clinically relevant setting, we used a recently
established patient-derived tumour-like cell cluster (PTC) system,
which faithfully recapitulates key genomic and drug-response features
of primary tumours, enabling personalized therapeutic assessment38
(Fig. 6a). We started by assessing the accessibility of virus-specific
T cells in patients with tumours. Focusing on the three most frequent
HLA alleles in the Chinese population, namely HLA-A*02:01, HLA-A*11:01
and HLA-A*24:02 (collectively accounting for over 50% allele frequency),
we selected 21 epitope peptides derived from CMV, EBV and influenza
virus, with seven peptides chosen specifically for each HLA allele
(Supplementary Table 6). We aimed to validate the response ratio of
virus-specific T cells in 28 tumour patients expressing these alleles
by quantifying IFNγ secretion after the stimulation by each peptide
(10 μg ml−1) for 6–9 days. Bystander antiviral T cells in patients with all
three HLA subtypes showed reactivity to the viral epitope pools (Fig. 6b
and Extended Data Fig. 9). Notably, HLA-A*02:01-positive patients had
an 80% response rate to the CMV epitope 02C1 (NLVPMVATV, NLVpep).
On the basis of these response profiles, we constructed HLA-matched
iVACs (iVAC-0201, iVAC-1101, iVAC-2402) using the epitopes with the
highest response for each allele.
These iVACs were applied to HLA-matched PTC models. Immunofluorescence analysis of colorectal cancer PTCs derived from patient 1
revealed that iVAC treatment induced significant intratumoural T cell
clonal expansion, GZMB secretion as well as decreased epithelial cell
density (Fig. 6c), contrasting to the minimal-to-absent responses
observed in control groups (epitope peptide alone or GlueBody2–CPP
degrader). This indicates successful redirection of CMV-reactive T cells
against tumours. iVAC also enhanced the cytokine secretion of TNF and
IFNγ, correlating with reduced PTC viability (Extended Data Fig. 10a).
Comparable results were observed in renal cell carcinoma PTCs from
patient 2, confirming iVAC-stimulated, virus-specific T-cell-mediated
tumour killing (Fig. 6d). Extended analysis of PTC viability in additional
patient-derived models (Fig. 6e and Extended Data Fig. 10b) revealed an
overall tumour response rate of 61% (17 out of 28). Stratified by HLA allele,
efficacy rates were 69% (9 out of 13) for HLA-A*02:01, 62.5% (5 out of 8) for
HLA-A*11:01 and 40% (4 out of 10) for HLA-A*24:02, respectively, which was
consistent with previous T cell response data. We further examined the
correlation between the PD-L1 expression levels of tumour cells and iVAC
efficacy, which indicated that elevated PD-L1 expression correlates with
enhanced treatment response (Extended Data Fig. 10c,d). These results
established that iVAC effectively redirects virus-antigen-responsive
T cells to eliminate tumour cells, demonstrating potent anti-tumour
activity and therapeutic potential in clinically relevant settings.

Oncolytic viruses represent a promising in  situ vaccination
approach using whole tumour cells as antigen sources39, yet safety
concerns arise from potential viral replication in normal tissues and
distracting antiviral responses that may overshadow tumour-specific
antigens40. Oncolytic viruses often induce PD-L1 overexpression, further dampening T cell activity41–43. In contrast to oncolytic viruses, iVAC
uses reprogrammed APC-like tumour cells to synergize checkpoint
degradation with direct T cell activation inside tumour. The generated tumour-specific immune responses may derive from two main
sources: (1) reactivation and redirection of bystander memory T cells
within the TME or recruited from peripheral circulation to eradicate
tumour cells; and (2) the induction of immunogenic cell death, which
releases tumour antigens to further stimulate tumour-specific T cell
responses.
Reduced antigen presentation is a known immune evasion mechanism in tumours, and exogenous antigen presentation by cancer cells
is rare, typically occurring only during infections that increase tumour
immunogenicity44. Higher tumour cell immunogenicity correlates with
better patient responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors, as immune
checkpoint blockade therapy reactivates suppressed T cells across
various cancers. Recent transmembrane and extracellular targeted
protein degradation strategies have shown promise by irreversibly
clearing immune checkpoints like PD-L1, offering enhanced efficacy
over conventional immune checkpoint inhibitors45–49. However, the
small population of tumour-specific T cells within tumours often exists
in an exhausted state, while more abundant bystander T cells, which
recognize non-tumour antigens, have greater potency18,21,43. Previous
attempts to redirect these bystander T cells through viral epitope delivery achieved limited success due to inefficiencies in epitope loading as
well as the lack of regulation over intrinsic signalling pathways50–52. By
contrast, iVAC uses natural endocytic pathways to efficiently present
exogenous peptides on MHC-I while concurrently degrading immune
checkpoints, enhancing tumour immunogenicity, activating IFN signalling and remodelling the TME. This strategy further boosts MHC-I
expression, rescues tumour antigen presentation, promotes cDC1
maturation and eliminates immunosuppressive microenvironment,
collectively driving a robust anti-tumour response.
Cellular reprogramming strategies hold promise for cancer therapy, with approaches targeting tumorigenicity reduction through
transcriptional reprogramming53–55. However, these methods often
have a low conversion efficiency, limiting their clinical use. Recent
studies have reprogrammed tumour cells into macrophages56 or
DCs23,24 through transcription factor manipulation, which enhanced
antigen presentation and CD8+
 T cell activation. However, such strategies have certain safety risks due to viral vectors, even when administered intratumourally, and the epigenetic reprogramming is slow
and asynchronous57–59. Alternatively, our chemical reprogramming
approach based on targeted membrane protein degradation rapidly
enhances tumour immunogenicity at the protein and immunopeptidome level. By directly initiating the APC-like remodelling of cancer cells within the tumour bed, iVAC may offer a general platform
for in situ activation of anti-tumour immunity across diverse patient
populations.

Methods
Cell line and primary cell culture
The human cancer MDA-MB-231, HCC2935, NCI-H1975, HCC827 and
mouse MC38 and B16 cells were purchased from ATCC or Procell. PD-L1
aAPC/CHO-K1 and PD-1 NFAT-luciferase/Jurkat cells were purchased
from Promega. Mouse PD-L1-knockout MC38 cells stably expressing
human PD-L1 (hPD-L1/MC38) were constructed by GemPharmatech. Mouse PD-L1-knockout MC38 cells expressing endogenous levels of human PD-L1 (hPD-L1endo/MC38) were generated by infecting
PD-L1-knockout MC38 cells with lentivirus encoding a chimeric PD-L1
construct consisting of the human PD-L1 extracellular domain and
the mouse PD-L1 intracellular domain followed by sorting based on
the surface PD-L1 level using flow cytometry. Mouse PD-L1-knockout
B16 cells expressing endogenous level human PD-L1 (hPD-L1/B16)
were constructed similarly to that of hPD-L1endo/MC38. All cell lines
were identified by short-tandem-repeat profiling, and were tested
and verified to be free of mycoplasma. HCC2935, NCI-H1975, HCC827
and hPD-L1/B16 cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco)
supplemented with 10% FBS (Invitrogen). MDA-MB-231 and MC38 cells
were maintained in DMEM medium (Gibco) supplemented with 10% FBS
(Invitrogen), and all of them were cultured in a humidified incubator at
37 °C under 5% CO2. hPD-L1/MC38 cells expressing luciferase were constructed by infecting hPD-L1/MC38 cells with lentivirus containing the
firefly luciferase gene followed by sorting based on puromycin resistance. OVA-specific naive CD8+
 T cells were isolated from the spleens of
female OT-1 mice that were 6–8 weeks old (Cyagen Biosciences) with the
negative CD8 isolation kit (StemCell Technologies, 19853) according to
manufacturer’s instructions. OVA-specific memory T cells were generated and cultured according to the literature60. CMV-specific TCR (NLV)
JC5 cells were a gift from M. M. Xu. Human PBMCs of healthy donors
were provided by Oribiotech, Shanghai, China (PB002-C). Detailed
information of healthy donors is summarized in Supplementary Table 7.
Mice
C57BL/6J PD-L1 humanized mice (hPD-L1/C57BL/6J) were purchased
from Shanghai Model Organisms Center. NCG mice were purchased
from GemPharmatech. All mice were maintained at 20–26 °C with
40–70% humidity under a 12 h–12 h light–dark cycle. All animal procedures were approved by the Animal Experimentation Ethics Committee
of Shenzhen Bay Laboratory (AECP202201) and were conducted in full
compliance with its guidelines, including the humane euthanasia of
mice when the tumour burden reached 10% of the body weight.
Patient samples
Tumour tissues and blood samples were obtained from patients undergoing tumour resection. Tumour tissues in the current study were
obtained across a broad spectrum of solid cancers, including colorectal
cancer (CRC), renal cell carcinoma (RCC), gastric carcinoma, breast
cancer and ovarian cancer. Detailed information of patients is provided
in Supplementary Table 8. This study was approved by the medical ethics committee of Peking University Cancer Hospital (NCT05280210),
Peking University People’s Hospital (NCT04130750). All of the patients
gave informed consent for collection of clinical information, tissue
collection and HLA-A typing.
Expression and purification of FnFSY incorporated GlueBodies
The plasmids pBAD-Nb-PD-L1-108TAG (Amp+
) and pSup-chFSYRS
V366A (Cm+
) were co-transformed into DH10B competent cells. For
incorporation of FnFSY into proteins, transformed bacteria were
cultured at 37 °C in 2× YT medium with 100 μg ml−1 ampicillin and
34 μg ml−1 chloramphenicol. When the OD600 reached 0.6, indicated
FnFSY was added into the medium at the final concentration of 1 mM
and induced with 0.2% arabinose when the optical density at 600 nm
(OD600) reached 1.0. After induction of expression at 30 °C for 12 h,
bacteria were collected by centrifugation at 4,500 rpm for 20 min at
4 °C. The cell pellets were resuspended in 10 ml lysis buffer (20 mM
Tris, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl) per g of wet weight. The cell suspension
was lysed in an ice-water bath for 20 min by sonication. The cell lysate
was centrifuged at 20,000g for 30 min at 4 °C and the supernatant was
loaded onto the Ni-NTA column (HisTrap HP, 5 ml, GE Healthcare) and
washed with 10 column volumes of wash buffer (20 mM Tris, pH 8.0,
150 mM NaCl, 50 mM imidazole). The protein was then eluted with
elution buffer (20 mM Tris, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl, 250 mM imidazole). The eluted protein solution was concentrated by ultrafiltration centrifugation (Millipore, UFC900324; MWCO, 3 kDa) and then
further purified by size-exclusion chromatography on the Superdex
75 10/300 GL (GE Healthcare) column equilibrated with PBS immediately. The protein concentration was measured using the NanoDrop
2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) based on the molecular mass and
extinction coefficient.
Sortase-A-mediated conjugation
Recombinant mgSrtA was expressed and purified as described previously61. The conjugation reactions between GlueBodies with a
C-terminal LPETGG-His6 sequence and substrate peptides with a
N-terminal GGG sequence were conducted by incubating 50 μM proteins (1 equiv.), 500 μM substrate peptides (10 equiv.) and 5 μM mgSrtA
(0.1 equiv.) in reaction buffer (1 mM CaCl2 in Tris, pH 7.8) for 1 h at room
temperature. Terminator MTSET (2 mM) was added into the reaction
mixture with a further 10 min incubation at room temperature followed
by desalting with Micro Bio-Spin P-6 Gel Columns (Bio-Rad, 7326221).
The unreacted proteins and mgSrtA were removed from the desired
products by magnetic Ni-NTA beads (Promega, V8565).
Analysis of PD-L1 degradation in vitro
Adherent cells (70% confluency) were treated with HBSS, GlueBody–
CPP, GlueBody2–CPP or iVAC in complete growth medium. At the indicated time, the growth medium was removed and the cells were lysed on
ice by adding cold 60 μl RIPA buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 89901)
with 1× protease inhibitor cocktail (CST, 5872). The protein concentration was quantified using the BCA protein assay kit (Thermo Fisher
Scientific, 23222). The samples were then heated at 95 °C for 20 min
after adding 5× reducing loading buffer (CWBio, CW0027). The denatured samples were analysed by electrophoresis in 4–20% SDS–PAGE
followed by western blot. For western blots shown in figures, the following antibodies were used at the indicated dilutions: human PD-L1
(CST, 13684, 1:1,000), GAPDH (Abcam, ab128915, 1:1,000) and β-actin
(CST, 4970, 1:1,000), GFP tag (CST, 2956, 1:1,000) and goat anti-rabbit
IgG, HRP-linked antibody (CST, 7074, 1:5,000) or horse anti-mouse IgG,
HRP-linked antibody (CST, 7076, 1:5,000).
PD-L1 degradation induces restored T cell activity in vitro
PD-1 NFAT-luciferase/Jurkat cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium
with 10% FBS, 1% penicillin–streptomycin, and 200 μg ml−1 of hygromycin B. PD-L1 aAPC/CHO-K1 cells were cultured in Ham’s F12 with 10%
FBS, 1% penicillin–streptomycin, 1 mg ml−1 Geneticin and 500 μg ml−1
hygromycin B (growth medium). To perform the in vitro efficacy assay,
PD-L1 aAPC/CHO-K1 cells were seeded at a density of 20,000 cells per
well in a white 96-well microplate with 100 μl of growth medium. After
cell attachment, the medium was removed from the CHO-K1 cells, and
cells were incubated with the fresh growth medium supplemented
with serially diluted GlueBody–CPP, GlueBody2–CPP or iVAC for 4 h.
After treatment, the medium was removed and cells were washed twice
with PBS followed by adding 30,000 PD-1 NFAT-luciferase/Jurkat cells

Construction of FRET-based iVAC
The preparation of fluorescent drug molecules for imaging follows a
strategy of first attaching fluorescent molecules to peptides (H-GGG-
(D-R)9-GGGS-K(N3)-V-Cit-C-SIINFEKL-OH) and then conjugating the
fluorescent peptides to GlueBodies with sortase-A-mediated ligation. For Cy3 signal, maleimide was chosen as the handle for ligation. The Cy3-conjugated peptide was obtained by incubating the
cysteine-bearing peptide (1 equiv.) with the Cy3-maleimide (5 equiv.)
for 60 min at room temperature in reaction buffer (double-distilled
H2O with 10% DMSO). To further introduce Cy5 signal into the peptide, DBCO was chosen as another handle for dye attachment. The
final FRET peptide with both Cy3 and Cy5 was obtained by incubating the Cy3-conjugated peptide including azido lysine (1 equiv.)
with the Cy5-DBCO molecule (10 equiv.) for 2 h at 37 °C in reaction
buffer. To prevent excess Cy3-maleimide molecules from reacting
with the sortase A, an excess of N-cysteine (10 equiv.) was added to
the system to quench the free maleimide moiety. The conjugation
between FRET peptide and GlueBodies was conducted according to
the mgSrtA-mediated peptide ligation process mentioned above. All
excess dye molecules in the system were removed during the desalting procedure.

RNA-seq analysis
Paired-end reads were aligned to the mouse genome (Ensembl, release
113) using HISAT v.2.2.0 using the default parameters. Gene counts
from this alignment were processed with the R package EdgeR, where
normalization was performed using the counts per million and the
trimmed mean of M-value methods. Differential expression analysis
was subsequently conducted with EdgeR, applying the limma-based
approach. Genes were considered upregulated when exhibiting a fold
change (FC) > 0 with an adjusted P value (Padj) <0.05, as determined by
the Benjamini-Hochberg correction. Conversely, downregulated genes
were defined by an FC < 0 and Padj < 0.05. The normalized counts were
then log2-transformed and visualized using either the ggplot2 package
in R or GraphPad Prism v.10.

The acquisition of immunopeptidome MS data
MHC-I peptide complexes were enriched and eluted using the NEODiscovery MHC-I Immuno-peptide Enrichment Kit (Baizhen Biotechnologies). In brief, cells were lysed in lysis buffer, incubated on ice
for 60 min, and sonicated for 5 min. After high-speed centrifugation,
the supernatant was collected for immunopeptidomics enrichment.
MHC–peptide complexes were purified by immunoprecipitation and
separated on a reversed-phase column (2215-C18, CDS Analytical) to
isolate MHC-I peptides, which were then analysed by liquid chromatography coupled with tandem MS (LC–MS/MS). Peptide samples,
redissolved in formic acid solution (A117-50, Thermo Fisher Scientific), were separated on a Bruker Nano-Elute nano-UPLC system with
a column temperature of 60 °C and a flow rate of 0.3 µl min−1. Mobile
phase A was 0.1% formic acid in water, purified using the Direct-Q
5UV system, and mobile phase B was 0.1% formic acid in acetonitrile
(A889-4L, Sigma-Aldrich). The gradient started at 96% A and 4% B held
for 48 min; followed by 80% A/20% B at 48 min; 70% A/30% B at 50 min;
and 0% A/100% B from 55 to 60 min. MS data were acquired on a Bruker
timsTOF Pro2 high-resolution mass spectrometer in data-dependent
acquisition mode over 60 min, with an m/z scan range of 100–1,700.
The parallel accumulation–serial fragmentation mode was configured for 10 MS/MS scans per cycle with a 2.27 s cycle time and an ion
intensity threshold of 1,000. Raw data files were generated in .d format
for analysis.
Immunopeptidome MS data processing
All raw data files were analysed using the DeepImmu Neoantigen Analysis Platform (Baizhen Biotechnologies), which integrates de novo
sequencing, database searching and homology searching for peptide
identification. Peptides of 7–16 amino acids in length were selected. This
platform identifies both canonical immunopeptides matching database
entries and non-canonical immunopeptides, including single-point
mutations. Database searching was performed using the mouse UniProt
database supplemented with OVA peptide sequence (SIINFEKL), with
the following parameters: no enzyme specified, a precursor tolerance
of 20.0 ppm, a fragment tolerance of 0.05 Da and variable oxidation
(+15.99 Da) on methionine. The peptide false-discovery rate was set to
1% for high-confidence identification. As for human cancer cell samples, the database search was performed against the human UniProt
database supplemented with CMV peptide sequences (NLVPMVATV).
The search parameters were the same.

Methodology
Sample preparation For flow cytometry analysis of the TME, tumors were isolated between day 15-18 post-injection, cut into pieces, and digested
for 30 min at 37 °C. Digested tumors were strained through a 70 μm filter and resuspended in FACS buffer (2% FBS, 2 mM
EDTA). FcR-block was performed with TruStain FcX anti-mouse CD16/32 for 15 min at 4 °C. For intracellular staining, first
perform cell surface antigen staining, then fix cells in 0.5 ml/tube Fixation Buffer (BioLegend) in the dark for 20 minutes at
room temperature followed by permeabilization.
Instrument LSRFortessa Cell Analyzer (BD Biosciences).
Software FACS Diva were used for data collection, data analysis was performed using the FlowJo software package.
Cell population abundance The manuscript presents the frequency or number of cell populations measured in all experiments.
Gating strategy Gating was based on FSC and SSC area, followed by dead cell removal using a Zombie stain.
